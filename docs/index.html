a · abstract

Predict in representation space, plan in imagination.

LeWM-Chess is a single end-to-end network ViT-Tiny encoder, AdaLN-zero autoregressive predictor, legality-masked policy head, bounded value head trained on 942,240 positions rendered from 10,000 elite games. It builds on the LeWM architecture (arXiv:2603.19312) and Yann LeCun's JEPA program: rather than reconstructing pixels, the model predicts its own future embeddings, conditioned on the action taken.

The result behaves like a world model, not a move classifier. Given the true move, next-state prediction error is 0.062; given a different-but-legal move it explodes to 0.433 a +0.370 causal planning gap. The policy head never proposes an illegal move, matches Stockfish's top choice 38% of the time, and its median chosen move costs just 12 centipawns. All from images.

b · why a world model

Engines search the game. This model searches its head.

Classical engines plan by querying a perfect simulator the rules of chess millions of times per second. Animals don't get a simulator. They build one: an internal model of how the world responds to their actions, then plan inside it.

That is the bet of the JEPA/world-model research program: learn the dynamics of the world in latent space, where irrelevant detail is already compressed away, and use the same representation for perception, prediction, and action.

Chess is the ideal stress test. The ground-truth simulator exists, so every claim of "understanding" can be audited: does the model's imagination match what actually happens on the board? Sections f–h answer that quantitatively.

What the model never sees

FEN strings · bitboards · legal-move generators · piece values · opening books · search trees. Its entire sensory world is a 128×128 image.

What it learns anyway

100% legal moves, Sicilian theory, action-conditioned dynamics with a 7.6× causal error ratio, and a value estimate of who is winning.

What it is not (yet)

A strong engine. It loses to full-strength Stockfish honestly shown in section i. The contribution is the world model, the substrate planning will run on.

c · architecture

One latent space. Three jobs: perceive, predict, act.

Press play to watch a 16-frame window flow through the network: the encoder turns each rendered board into a 256-d latent z, the move embedder injects the action, and the 8-block AdaLN-zero predictor rolls the future forward K=4 steps the same autoregressive computation used at inference, trained directly.

Components

Renderer → ViT-Tiny encoder

128×128 px · patch 16 · one square per patch

Pieces are drawn as twelve distinct colored disks, so each ViT patch contains exactly one square the cleanest possible visual tokenization of a board. A 192→2048→256 MLP projects into the shared latent space.

Move embedder

20,480-token vocab · from×64+to (+promotions) · game progress

A collision-free UCI vocabulary embeds each action, plus a learned encoding of game progress. This vector conditions every predictor block through AdaLN.

AdaLN-zero AR predictor

8 blocks · 16 heads · dim 256 · residual drift ẑ = z + Δ(h)

A causal transformer over the latent sequence, modulated by the action. The zero-initialized drift head means the model starts from "nothing changes" and learns only the delta a move causes.

Policy & value heads

legality-masked logits · tanh-bounded value

The same latents feed a 20k-way policy head (masked to legal moves at play time) and a tanh(cp/400)-style value head readouts of the world model, ready for an RL planning stage.

d · objective & training

Five losses, each aimed at a known way latent predictors die.

Multi-step rollout loss

Σₖ γᵏ⁻¹ ‖ẑt+k − zt+k‖² · K=4 · γ=0.9

Trains the deployed autoregressive computation, killing exposure bias. By Theorem 1, compounding error is bounded by ε·(Lᵏ−1)/(L−1).

Jacobian contraction penalty

β·‖Jv‖² via Hutchinson, β=10⁻³

One stochastic matvec per step pushes the latent map toward Lipschitz L ≤ 1 so rollout error grows linearly, not exponentially.

Scheduled sampling

DAgger-style · p: 0 → 0.5 over training

The predictor's own outputs are mixed back into its context, so it trains on the error distribution it will actually face at inference.

InfoNCE action contrast

true move vs. shuffled move · cosine logits

Different moves must produce distinguishable latent futures exactly the signal a planner needs to compare actions.

SIGReg anti-collapse

Epps–Pulley ECF statistic · 1024 random projections

Regularizes every 1-D projection of the embedding toward a standard Gaussian. Result: 0 dead dimensions out of 256 (section h).

Policy + value readouts

0.2·CE(policy) + 0.05·MSE(WDL)

Light supervised heads keep the latent space action-relevant and outcome-aware without dominating the world-model objective.

e · beyond lewm

Base LeWM predicts one step. Chess punishes that.

The base architecture is trained teacher-forced: predict zt+1 from the true zt. But at planning time the model must run on its own outputs and any per-step error ε compounds through the latent map's Lipschitz constant L as ε·(Lᵏ−1)/(L−1). If L > 1, imagination explodes exponentially. Four upgrades attack exactly this.

Theorem 1, live the Hutchinson Jacobian contraction penalty

A single stochastic Jacobian-vector product per step adds β·‖Jzv‖² to the loss, squeezing the predictor's Lipschitz constant toward L ≤ 1. Drag L to see why an unregularized rollout dies and where ours lives.
L = 0.98 ε = 0.062 the model's measured 1-step error

The lavender dots are the measured closed-loop drift from section g. At L ≈ 0.98 the theoretical bound saturates near ε/(1−L) ≈ 0.65 almost exactly the plateau the trained model exhibits (0.62). The penalty isn't decoration; it predicts the model's behavior.

Scheduled sampling (DAgger-style)

f · action causality

Does it understand moves, or just memorize trajectories?

The defining test of a world model: predict the next latent state given the true move, a shuffled (different but legal) move, pure noise, and a no-change null. A trajectory-memorizer scores the same everywhere. A causal model collapses only when given the truth.

Action-conditional prediction error (latent MSE, 78k held-out windows)

The ordering carries a subtle signature of real modeling: a wrong-but-legal move hurts more than random noise (0.433 vs 0.390). A coherent false premise drags the imagined future onto the wrong branch of the game tree; incoherent garbage can be partially ignored. The model has learned the manifold of plausible futures.

g · imagination drift

Closed-loop rollouts: feeding the model its own dreams.

From the starting position, the predictor is rolled forward on its own outputs no ground-truth frames and compared against re-encoded reality at every step. This is the regime where unregularized latent models diverge exponentially.

LeWM-Chess drifts, then saturates near 0.62 latent MSE comfortably below the 0.43 error of a single wrong move compounding. The Jacobian contraction penalty is doing exactly what Theorem 1 promises: linear-not-exponential error growth, and a stable horizon of several plies of usable imagination.

That horizon is the budget a future latent-space MCTS gets to spend.

j · reproduce

One file trains it. Two more interrogate it.

# install
$ pip install -r requirements.txt

# 1 · render 942k positions into a uint8 mmap cache
$ python train.py --mode build-cache --pgn chessgames.pgn

# 2 · train (H100-tuned: bf16, fused AdamW, 16 workers)
$ python train.py --mode train --pgn chessgames.pgn \
      --epochs 10 --batch-size 256

# 3 · stockfish-grounded eval
$ python train.py --mode stockfish \
      --ckpt outputs/lewm_chess_best.pt

# 4 · world-model probes (sections f–h)
$ python diagnostics.py --ckpt outputs/lewm_chess_best.pt

# 5 · play it
$ python play.py --color white --depth 12

How it works

Your move is sent as a FEN string to the model's policy head. It picks the highest-scoring legal move from its 20k-vocabulary logit output no search, pure imagination.

The model was never told the rules of chess. Every legal-move guarantee comes from a legality mask applied to the raw logits at inference time.
